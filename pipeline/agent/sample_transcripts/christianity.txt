Christianity began as a movement within Judaism in the Roman province of Judea, centered on the teachings of Jesus of Nazareth. According to the tradition, his Crucifixion in Jerusalem around 30 CE became the founding event of the new faith, and the conviction of his followers that he had risen transformed a small Galilean sect into a missionary religion. The figure most responsible for carrying that message beyond Judea was the apostle Paul, whose missionary journeys across the eastern Mediterranean between roughly 47 CE and 57 CE planted communities in cities such as Antioch, Corinth, and Ephesus. It was in Antioch that the followers of Jesus were first called Christians, and Paul's letters became the earliest writings of what would grow into the Christian scriptures.

For its first centuries Christianity spread despite recurring persecution, most violently under the Great Persecution launched by the emperor Diocletian in 303 CE. The decisive turn came with Constantine, who issued the Edict of Milan in 313 CE, granting toleration to Christians across the Roman Empire. Seeking to settle a bitter dispute with Arianism over the nature of Christ, Constantine convened the Council of Nicaea in 325 CE in the city of Nicaea, which produced the Nicene Creed and condemned the teaching of Arius. The faith's rise reached its climax when the emperor Theodosius issued the Edict of Thessalonica in 380 CE, making Nicene Christianity the official state religion of the Roman Empire and setting the stage for the suppression of rival cults.

As the religion grew, so did the need to define doctrine and translate scripture. The scholar Jerome completed the Vulgate, his Latin translation of the Bible, around 405 CE, giving the Western Church its standard text for a thousand years. The Council of Chalcedon in 451 CE defined the orthodox understanding of Christ's two natures, but in doing so it fractured the eastern churches, as the Oriental Orthodox communions rejected its formula. Meanwhile in Rome, Pope Leo I asserted the special authority of the bishop of Rome, laying foundations for the later claims of the papacy that would shape Catholicism for centuries.

The long drift between the Latin West and the Greek East culminated in the Great Schism of 1054 CE, when mutual excommunications split Christianity into the Roman Catholicism of the West and the Eastern Orthodoxy centered on Constantinople. Religious energy in the West soon turned outward when Pope Urban II preached the First Crusade in 1095 CE, launching centuries of armed expeditions to seize the Holy Land from Jerusalem. The papacy's reach over belief hardened further with the founding of the Medieval Inquisition around 1184 CE, established to root out heresy such as that of the Cathars in southern France.

The later medieval centuries brought crisis and division to the institutional Church. The Avignon Papacy began in 1309 CE, when the popes relocated from Rome to Avignon under French influence, and the discredit deepened with the Western Schism of 1378 CE, during which rival popes claimed legitimacy at once. The Fall of Constantinople to the Ottomans in 1453 CE ended the Byzantine Empire and scattered Greek scholars westward, an exodus that fed the intellectual ferment of the age. By then calls for reform were mounting against the sale of indulgences and the worldliness of the hierarchy.

The breaking point came in 1517 CE, when Martin Luther posted his Ninety-five Theses in Wittenberg, igniting the Protestant Reformation and the birth of Protestantism. Luther was excommunicated and defied the emperor at the Diet of Worms in 1521 CE, while reformers such as John Calvin and Huldrych Zwingli built rival movements across northern Europe. The Roman Church answered with its own Counter-Reformation, formally launched at the Council of Trent, which opened in 1545 CE and reaffirmed Catholic doctrine while curbing the worst abuses. By the mid-sixteenth century, the single Western Church of medieval Europe had shattered into the competing confessions that would define Christianity for the modern era.
