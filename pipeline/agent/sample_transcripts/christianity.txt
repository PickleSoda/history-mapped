Christianity emerged in the first century CE in Roman Judea, founded on the teachings of Jesus of Nazareth,
who was crucified in Jerusalem around 30 CE. The apostle Paul of Tarsus spread the new faith across the
Roman Empire, writing epistles to early Christian communities in Corinth, Ephesus, and Rome. For nearly
three centuries Christians faced sporadic persecution under emperors such as Nero and Diocletian.

This changed when Emperor Constantine the Great issued the Edict of Milan in 313 CE, granting religious
tolerance throughout the empire. In 325 CE Constantine convened the First Council of Nicaea, which produced
the Nicene Creed and condemned the teachings of Arius known as Arianism. Christianity became the official
state religion of the Roman Empire under Emperor Theodosius I in 380 CE.

In 1054 CE the Great Schism split the church into the Roman Catholic Church in the west, led by the Pope in
Rome, and the Eastern Orthodox Church, centered on Constantinople. Nearly five centuries later, in 1517 CE,
the German monk Martin Luther launched the Protestant Reformation by posting his Ninety-five Theses in
Wittenberg, challenging the authority of the papacy and fracturing Western Christianity into competing
denominations.
