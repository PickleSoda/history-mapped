The Mongol Empire grew from the steppes of Central Asia into the largest contiguous land empire in human history, a transformation set in motion by a single man. Born Temujin around 1162 CE, he spent decades fighting rival clans before subduing the warring tribes of the Mongolian plateau. In 1206 CE a great assembly, the kurultai, proclaimed him Genghis Khan, supreme ruler of all the Mongols. He reorganized his followers into a disciplined decimal army and established the city of Karakorum as the heart of his domain. The Mongol cavalry he forged, mounted archers capable of covering vast distances, would soon terrify settled civilizations from China to the gates of Europe.

Genghis Khan first turned against the Jin Dynasty of northern China, and in 1215 CE his horde captured the great city of Zhongdu, near modern Beijing. He then looked west toward the wealthy Khwarazmian Empire of Persia and Central Asia. After Khwarazmian governors murdered Mongol envoys and merchants, Genghis launched a campaign of annihilation. In 1220 CE the legendary city of Samarkand fell, followed by Bukhara, while his general Subutai swept across Persia and around the Caspian Sea. The Mongols extinguished the Khwarazmian state, and Genghis Khan died in 1227 CE during a campaign against the Tangut Xi Xia kingdom, leaving an empire that already stretched across much of Asia.

His son Ogedei Khan, elected in 1229 CE, expanded the conquests and built up Karakorum as a true imperial capital. Under Ogedei the Mongols completed the destruction of the Jin Dynasty in 1234 CE and unleashed Subutai and the prince Batu on a devastating European campaign. The army sacked Kiev in 1240 CE, and in 1241 CE it crushed a combined Polish and German force at the Battle of Legnica and shattered the Hungarian army at the Battle of Mohi. Europe seemed open to conquest, but the death of Ogedei Khan in 1241 CE drew the princes homeward to elect a successor, and the western advance halted.

The empire's reach continued under later khans. Hulagu Khan led a fresh assault into the Middle East, and in 1258 CE he besieged and sacked Baghdad, executing the last Abbasid caliph and ending five centuries of Abbasid rule. From these conquests Hulagu founded the Ilkhanate in Persia, while the descendants of Batu ruled the Russian steppes as the Golden Horde. In the east, Genghis Khan's grandson Kublai Khan rose to power, and in 1271 CE he proclaimed the Yuan Dynasty, formally founding a Chinese imperial house under Mongol rule with its capital at Khanbaliq, present-day Beijing. By 1279 CE Kublai had completed the conquest of the Song Dynasty of southern China, uniting all of China under Mongol authority for the first time.

Not every venture succeeded. Kublai Khan twice tried to invade Japan, and both fleets met disaster, the second in 1281 CE when a great typhoon the Japanese called the kamikaze, or divine wind, wrecked the Mongol armada. Yet across the conquered lands the period known as the Pax Mongolica brought unprecedented stability to Eurasia. Caravans moved safely along the Silk Road, and goods, ideas, and travelers flowed between China and the Mediterranean. It was during this era that the Venetian merchant Marco Polo journeyed to Kublai's court, departing for home around 1295 CE with tales that astonished Europe and fed its hunger for eastern trade.

The unity of the empire could not endure across such distances. After the death of the last great khan it fragmented through diplomatic division and rivalry into four major khanates: the Yuan Dynasty in China, the Ilkhanate in Persia, the Golden Horde on the Russian steppe, and the Chagatai Khanate in Central Asia, each governed by competing branches of Genghis Khan's line. The Ilkhanate dissolved by 1335 CE, and in 1368 CE a Chinese rebellion overthrew the Yuan Dynasty and established the Ming. Though the political empire splintered, its legacy endured in the trade networks it secured, the exchange of cultures it enabled, and the enduring memory of the horsemen who once ruled from the Pacific to the plains of Hungary.
