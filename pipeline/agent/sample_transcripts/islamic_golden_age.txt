The Islamic Golden Age unfolded across roughly five centuries, beginning in earnest when the Abbasid Caliphate seized power and the caliph al-Mansur resolved to build a new capital worthy of a vast empire. In 762 CE he founded Baghdad on the banks of the Tigris, laying out a circular city that within a generation became the largest metropolis on earth. The Abbasids ruled in the name of the Quran, yet they actively gathered the learning of Persia, India, Greece, and beyond, and Baghdad swiftly eclipsed older centers such as Damascus, the former Umayyad seat. A pivotal moment came in 751 CE, when, according to tradition, the secret of paper-making passed from captured Chinese artisans after the Battle of Talas; within decades the first paper mill in the Muslim world opened in Baghdad around 794 CE, and cheap paper transformed the spread of knowledge across the empire.

The intellectual heart of this era was the House of Wisdom, an academy of scholarship and translation that flourished under caliphal patronage. Though its roots lay in al-Mansur's reign, it reached its zenith under the caliph al-Mamun, who expanded it dramatically after 813 CE and made Baghdad the engine of the great translation movement. There, Greek, Syriac, Persian, and Sanskrit works on medicine, astronomy, and philosophy were rendered into Arabic, preserving texts that might otherwise have vanished. Among the academy's brightest minds was Muhammad ibn Musa al-Khwarizmi, who around 820 CE composed his foundational treatise on algebra, "al-Kitab al-mukhtasar fi hisab al-jabr wal-muqabala," giving both the discipline and the word "algorithm" their names. Astronomers of the same generation refined the astrolabe, an instrument inherited from the Greeks and perfected in the Islamic world, which let scholars measure the heavens and determine the direction of prayer with unprecedented precision.

Medicine advanced as boldly as mathematics. The physician Abu Bakr al-Razi, known to the Latin West as Rhazes, directed hospitals in Baghdad and, around 900 CE, wrote groundbreaking clinical works including his treatise distinguishing smallpox from measles, along with his vast medical encyclopedia, the "Kitab al-Hawi." A century later, the Persian polymath Ibn Sina, whom Europeans called Avicenna, completed his monumental "Canon of Medicine" around 1025 CE, a five-volume synthesis that would dominate medical teaching in both the Islamic world and Europe for some six hundred years. Scholarship was not confined to the east: in 859 CE, in the city of Fez, a woman named Fatima al-Fihri founded al-Qarawiyyin, an educational institution often celebrated as the oldest continuously operating degree-granting university in the world, drawing students from across North Africa and Iberia.

The pursuit of natural philosophy produced perhaps the era's most rigorous scientist in Ibn al-Haytham, known in Latin as Alhazen, who worked chiefly in Cairo under Fatimid patronage. Around 1021 CE he completed his seven-volume "Book of Optics," overturning ancient theories of vision and pioneering an experimental method that anticipated the scientific approach by centuries. Cairo itself, founded by the Fatimids in 969 CE, became a rival capital of learning, home to the al-Azhar mosque-university established shortly afterward. In Persia, the polymath Omar Khayyam reformed the calendar with extraordinary accuracy through the Jalali reform of 1079 CE, made original contributions to algebra, and composed the verses later gathered as the "Rubaiyat," for which he remains best loved in the wider world.

The far western frontier of Islam, al-Andalus, rivaled the east in splendor, with Cordoba growing under Umayyad rule into one of Europe's greatest cities. The Caliphate of Cordoba was proclaimed in 929 CE, and its libraries reportedly held hundreds of thousands of volumes. There the philosopher Ibn Rushd, known to Latin Christendom as Averroes, wrote his influential commentaries on Aristotle around 1180 CE, works that would profoundly shape medieval European thought when translated into Latin and Hebrew. Cordoba's translators and scholars became a bridge through which classical and Arabic learning flowed northward into Christian Europe, seeding the later Renaissance.

The golden age did not end in a single stroke, but its eastern heart was shattered in 1258 CE, when the Mongol armies of Hulagu Khan besieged and sacked Baghdad, executing the last Abbasid caliph and, according to chroniclers, casting so many books from the House of Wisdom into the Tigris that the river ran black with ink. Though learning continued in Cairo, Damascus, Granada, and the rising Persian and Ottoman courts, the destruction of Baghdad marked the symbolic close of an extraordinary age in which the inheritance of the ancient world was preserved, expanded, and transmitted to the centuries that followed.
