The Islamic Golden Age was a period of flourishing science, culture, and learning across the Muslim world,
conventionally dated from the eighth to the thirteenth century CE. It began under the Abbasid Caliphate after
the capital was moved to Baghdad, founded in 762 CE by the caliph al-Mansur. In Baghdad the caliph al-Mamun
established the House of Wisdom, a great center of translation and research, in the early ninth century.

Scholars there translated the works of Greek thinkers such as Aristotle and Ptolemy into Arabic. The
mathematician al-Khwarizmi, working in the House of Wisdom around 820 CE, wrote a foundational treatise that
gave its name to algebra and introduced the Hindu-Arabic numeral system to the Islamic world. The physician
Ibn Sina, known in the west as Avicenna, wrote The Canon of Medicine around 1025 CE, a text used in European
universities for centuries. The polymath Ibn al-Haytham, or Alhazen, produced the Book of Optics in Cairo
around 1021 CE, laying foundations for the scientific method.

The age was disrupted when the Mongol Empire under Hulagu Khan sacked Baghdad in 1258 CE, destroying the
House of Wisdom and ending Abbasid power. Centers of learning nonetheless persisted in cities such as Cordoba
and Cairo, transmitting classical and Islamic scholarship to Renaissance Europe.
