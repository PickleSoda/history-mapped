Classical Greek philosophy laid the foundations of Western thought, centered on Athens in the fifth and
fourth centuries BCE. Socrates, who taught in Athens until his execution in 399 BCE, pioneered a method of
questioning that bears his name, though he wrote nothing himself. His pupil Plato founded the Academy in
Athens around 387 BCE and wrote dialogues such as the Republic, exploring justice and the ideal state.

Plato's own student Aristotle founded the Lyceum in Athens in 335 BCE and produced works on logic, biology,
ethics, and politics that shaped scholarship for two thousand years. Aristotle tutored the young Alexander
the Great before Alexander's conquests spread Greek culture across the Near East, beginning the Hellenistic
age.

Earlier thinkers had prepared the ground: Pythagoras founded a philosophical and mathematical school in the
sixth century BCE, and Thales of Miletus is often called the first philosopher for seeking natural rather
than mythological explanations. After the classical period, schools such as Stoicism, founded by Zeno of
Citium around 300 BCE, and Epicureanism continued to shape ethics well into the Roman era.
