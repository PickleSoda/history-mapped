Ancient Greek philosophy began not in Athens but on the Ionian coast, in the bustling commercial city of Miletus, where Thales sought to explain the cosmos through natural principles rather than myth. Tradition credits him with predicting a solar eclipse in 585 BCE, an event so striking that it reportedly halted a battle between the Lydians and the Medes and earned Thales lasting fame as the first of the Pre-Socratics. His successors in the Milesian school, Anaximander and Anaximenes, pushed these inquiries further through the sixth century BCE. Around 530 BCE, Pythagoras left the island of Samos and founded his school at Croton in southern Italy, a community devoted to mathematics, music, and the transmigration of the soul. At nearby Ephesus, Heraclitus composed his famously obscure aphorisms around 500 BCE, teaching that all things are in flux, while Parmenides of Elea argued the opposite around 480 BCE, that change itself is an illusion.

The intellectual center of gravity then shifted decisively to Athens during its golden age. Socrates, who served as a hoplite at the Battle of Potidaea in 432 BCE, spent his life questioning his fellow citizens in the agora, claiming to know nothing yet exposing the ignorance of those who claimed wisdom. He wrote nothing himself, but his method transformed philosophy from speculation about nature into a relentless examination of ethics and the human soul. In 399 BCE the Athenian democracy put him on trial for impiety and corrupting the youth; condemned by the jury, he drank hemlock and died, an event that haunted his followers for generations and made him philosophy's first martyr.

Socrates' devoted pupil Plato carried his teacher's legacy forward. After years of travel, Plato founded the Academy in Athens around 387 BCE, an institution often called the first university of the Western world, which would endure for centuries. There he composed the great dialogues, including the Republic, which set out his vision of justice, the philosopher-king, and the theory of Forms, alongside works such as the Phaedo and the Symposium. The Academy attracted students from across the Greek world, among them a young man from Stagira in northern Greece named Aristotle, who arrived around 367 BCE and remained for two decades until Plato's death in 347 BCE.

Aristotle then embarked on a remarkable career of his own. Around 343 BCE, King Philip II of Macedon summoned him to tutor his son, the future Alexander the Great, an arrangement that linked the most influential philosopher of the age to the most consequential conqueror. Returning to Athens, Aristotle founded the Lyceum in 335 BCE, where he and his peripatetic followers produced an astonishing body of work on logic, biology, physics, metaphysics, ethics, and politics that would dominate scholarship for nearly two thousand years. When Alexander died in 323 BCE, a wave of anti-Macedonian feeling swept Athens, and Aristotle fled the city, declaring that he would not let Athens sin twice against philosophy; he died the following year in 322 BCE.

Alexander's conquests scattered Greek language and ideas across the Near East, opening the Hellenistic age in which new schools competed for the loyalty of a more cosmopolitan world. Around 307 BCE, Epicurus established his community known as the Garden in Athens, teaching that pleasure understood as tranquility and the absence of pain was the highest good, and giving rise to the movement of Epicureanism. Almost simultaneously, around 300 BCE, Zeno of Citium began lecturing in the painted colonnade called the Stoa Poikile, founding Stoicism, which held that virtue and reason in accordance with nature were the path to a good life. The Cynics, embodied by the provocative Diogenes of Sinope who had scandalized Athens earlier in the fourth century BCE, continued to challenge convention and helped inspire the Stoics.

These traditions long outlived their founders and shaped the wider Mediterranean. As Rome expanded eastward, it absorbed Greek learning wholesale; the philosophical embassy to Rome in 155 BCE, which included the Academic skeptic Carneades, dazzled Roman audiences and quickened the spread of Platonism, Stoicism, and Epicureanism into Latin culture. By the first century BCE, Roman thinkers such as Cicero and Lucretius were transmitting Greek philosophy to a new civilization, ensuring that the inquiries begun at Miletus would echo across the centuries to come.
