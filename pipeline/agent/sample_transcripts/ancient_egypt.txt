The civilization of ancient Egypt rose along the banks of the Nile, whose annual floods deposited the fertile black silt that made dense agriculture possible in an otherwise arid land. Around 3100 BCE the king known to history as Narmer unified Upper and Lower Egypt into a single realm, establishing his capital at Memphis and inaugurating the long line of pharaonic dynasties. It was in this Early Dynastic period that the hieroglyphic script matured into a full writing system, carved on monuments and palettes such as the famous Narmer Palette, while scribes also developed the cursive hieratic hand for everyday record-keeping on papyrus.

The Old Kingdom, beginning around 2686 BCE, was the great age of pyramid building. The architect Imhotep designed the Step Pyramid at Saqqara for the pharaoh Djoser around 2670 BCE, the first monumental structure built entirely of cut stone. A few generations later, around 2560 BCE, Khufu raised the Great Pyramid of Giza, the largest of the three Giza pyramids and the only surviving Wonder of the ancient world; his successors added the second pyramid and the Great Sphinx, that colossal lion-bodied guardian carved from the bedrock. These projects depended on enormous labor, on quarried limestone and granite, and on the gold flowing from mines in Nubia and the Eastern Desert. After the Old Kingdom collapsed into the First Intermediate Period around 2181 BCE, Egypt was reunified in the Middle Kingdom around 2055 BCE under rulers based at Thebes, an era remembered for its classic literature and renewed prosperity.

This stability faltered again, and during the Second Intermediate Period the Hyksos, a people of Levantine origin, seized control of the Nile Delta around 1650 BCE, ruling from their city of Avaris and introducing the horse-drawn chariot and the composite bow. The Theban prince Ahmose I expelled the Hyksos around 1550 BCE and founded the Eighteenth Dynasty, launching the New Kingdom, Egypt's imperial age. During this period the cult of Amun, centered on the vast temple complex of Karnak at Thebes, grew immensely wealthy and powerful as Egypt expanded its empire into Syria and Nubia.

The New Kingdom produced some of Egypt's most celebrated rulers. Hatshepsut, one of the few female pharaohs, reigned from about 1479 BCE to 1458 BCE, sponsoring a famous trading expedition to the land of Punt and building her elegant terraced mortuary temple at Deir el-Bahri. A few decades later, around 1353 BCE, Akhenaten launched a radical religious revolution, suppressing the traditional gods in favor of the sole worship of the sun-disk Aten, a movement known as Atenism, and moving his capital to a new city at Amarna. His successor, the boy-king Tutankhamun, reversed these reforms after taking the throne around 1332 BCE and restored the cult of Amun; he is best known today because his nearly intact tomb was discovered in 1922 CE.

The Nineteenth Dynasty brought Ramesses II, often called Ramesses the Great, who ruled from about 1279 BCE to 1213 BCE and fought the Hittites at the Battle of Kadesh around 1274 BCE, later concluding the earliest known peace treaty. He covered the land with monuments, including the rock-cut temples at Abu Simbel and additions to Karnak. After his long reign, Egypt entered a slow decline through the Third Intermediate Period beginning around 1069 BCE, suffering invasions and foreign rule by Libyans, Nubians, and Assyrians, and falling under Persian domination in 525 BCE.

Egypt's final chapter opened when Alexander the Great conquered the country in 332 BCE and founded the city of Alexandria, which became a renowned center of learning. After his death his general Ptolemy founded the Ptolemaic Dynasty, a Greek-speaking line of rulers who governed Egypt for nearly three centuries. The last of them, Cleopatra VII, allied herself with Julius Caesar and then Mark Antony, but after their defeat by Octavian she died in 30 BCE, whereupon Egypt was annexed as a province of the Roman Empire, ending three thousand years of pharaonic rule.
