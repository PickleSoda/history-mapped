The Age of Exploration began not with a voyage but with a school of patient ambition on the windswept Atlantic coast of Portugal. From the 1410s the prince known as Henry the Navigator gathered cartographers, shipwrights, and pilots at Sagres and sent caravels probing ever further down the African shore, and in 1419 his captains reached the Madeira islands. The decisive psychological barrier fell in 1434 when Gil Eanes finally rounded Cape Bojador, long feared as the edge of the navigable world, opening the West African coast to Portuguese trade in gold and, tragically, in enslaved people.

The momentum Henry set in motion outlived him. In 1488 Bartolomeu Dias was driven by storms around the southern tip of Africa, proving that the Indian Ocean could be reached by sea and naming the headland the Cape of Good Hope. While Portugal pressed south and east, a Genoese navigator named Christopher Columbus took a contrary idea to the rival court of Castile: that Asia could be reached by sailing west. Sponsored at last by the Kingdom of Spain, Columbus crossed the Atlantic in 1492 and made landfall in the Caribbean, convinced to his death that he had reached the Indies.

The sudden collision of two overseas empires forced a division of the globe. In 1494 the Treaty of Tordesillas, mediated by the Pope, drew a meridian through the Atlantic and split the unexplored world between Spain and Portugal, a line that would shape the languages of the Americas for centuries. Portugal's eastern wager paid off in 1498 when Vasco da Gama, rounding the Cape, reached Calicut on the coast of India and returned to Lisbon with a cargo of pepper and cinnamon that broke the Venetian and Arab monopoly on the spice trade.

What followed was conquest as much as commerce. In 1519 the conquistador Hernán Cortés landed in Mexico and within two years had toppled the Aztec Empire, seizing its capital Tenochtitlan in 1521. A decade later, in 1532, Francisco Pizarro captured the Inca emperor Atahualpa and shattered the Inca Empire, funneling staggering quantities of silver toward Seville. That same restless decade had already produced the supreme feat of seamanship: the expedition of Ferdinand Magellan, which set out from Spain in 1519 and, though Magellan himself was killed in the Philippines in 1521, completed the first circumnavigation of the globe in 1522 under Juan Sebastián Elcano.

Knowledge struggled to keep pace with the ships. The flood of new coastlines made older maps obsolete, and in 1507 the cartographer Martin Waldseemüller printed a world map that, crediting the navigator Amerigo Vespucci, labeled the new continent "America" for the first time. The intellectual upheaval reached its height in 1543, when Nicolaus Copernicus published "On the Revolutions of the Heavenly Spheres," displacing the Earth from the center of the cosmos even as explorers were redrawing its surface.

By the middle of the sixteenth century the maritime powers had bound the oceans into a single commercial web. The Manila galleon trade, inaugurated in 1565, carried Mexican silver across the Pacific to exchange for Chinese silk and porcelain, knitting Asia, the Americas, and Europe into the first truly global economy. The caravel and the carrack, the compass and the astrolabe, had remade the world — and the Silk Road's overland caravans gave way for good to the sea lanes that the explorers had charted.
