The Viking Age opened with a thunderclap of violence on the morning the longships beached at Lindisfarne. In 793 CE, Norse raiders fell upon the monastery off the coast of Northumbria, slaughtering monks and plundering the treasures of one of Christianity's most revered houses in the British Isles. To the men who came from Denmark and Norway, this was no sacrilege but opportunity; they carried with them the old gods of Norse paganism, a religious movement whose myths of Odin, Thor, and the doomed twilight of Ragnarok would one day be set down in the Poetic Edda and the Prose Edda. The shock of Lindisfarne rippled through the Christian world, and for the next three centuries the peoples of Scandinavia would raid, trade, conquer, and ultimately settle across an astonishing breadth of the known world.

The raids soon hardened into something more ambitious. In 841 CE, Norse warriors established a fortified longphort on the River Liffey, founding the settlement that would become Dublin and binding Ireland to the wider Norse trading world. In the same restless decades the Great Heathen Army descended on England, and by 878 CE, after Alfred the Great checked their advance at the Battle of Edington, a treaty carved out the Danelaw, the swath of eastern and northern England where Danish law and custom held sway. Across the Channel the Franks fared little better; in 845 CE a fleet under Ragnar sailed up the Seine and sacked Paris, and again in 885 CE a vast host laid the great siege of Paris that lasted nearly a year, a defining event that exposed the weakness of the West Frankish crown and helped, in 911 CE, persuade King Charles the Simple to grant Normandy to the chieftain Rollo.

While some Norsemen carved kingdoms from Christian Europe, others sailed west into the empty Atlantic. The settlement of Iceland began in earnest around 874 CE, when Ingolfr Arnarson made his home at Reykjavik, and by 930 CE the Icelandic settlers had founded the Althing, one of the world's oldest parliaments. From this rugged island sprang a remarkable literary culture, for it was here that the Icelandic sagas would later be composed in cold farmhouses through long winters. Pushing still farther, the outlaw Erik the Red discovered and began colonizing Greenland around 985 CE, giving the icy land a hopeful name to lure colonists. His son Leif Erikson sailed beyond even that frontier, reaching the shores of North America at Vinland around 1000 CE, an astonishing voyage to a continent unknown to medieval Europe and later confirmed by the Norse remains at L'Anse aux Meadows.

The same era saw the gods of the north begin to yield to the cross. In 965 CE the Danish king Harald Bluetooth accepted Christianity and proclaimed his conversion on the great runestone at Jelling, declaring that he had made the Danes Christian. The new faith spread unevenly; in 1000 CE the Althing of Iceland voted to adopt Christianity to avoid civil war, even as many quietly kept their old rites. Norway's own conversion was driven hard by kings such as Olaf Tryggvason and later Olaf Haraldsson, whose death at the Battle of Stiklestad in 1030 CE made him a saint and sealed the kingdom's Christian future.

The political ambitions of Scandinavia reached their height under Cnut the Great, who conquered England and by 1016 CE ruled a North Sea empire uniting England, Denmark, and Norway under a single crown. Yet the age that began with raiders would end with one final, fateful clash for the English throne. In 1066 CE the Norwegian king Harald Hardrada, last of the great Viking adventurers, invaded northern England and was killed by Harold Godwinson at the Battle of Stamford Bridge, a victory that left the exhausted English to face William of Normandy at Hastings only weeks later. Stamford Bridge is often remembered as the symbolic close of the Viking Age.

The memory of these centuries endured chiefly through the written word of Iceland. In the early thirteenth century, around 1220 CE, the chieftain and poet Snorri Sturluson compiled the Prose Edda, a handbook of Norse mythology and skaldic verse, and tradition credits him with the kings' saga known as Heimskringla as well. Through these cultural works, alongside the anonymous poems of the Poetic Edda gathered around 1270 CE, the beliefs, heroes, and history of the Norse world survived the triumph of Christianity to shape the imagination of every age that followed.
