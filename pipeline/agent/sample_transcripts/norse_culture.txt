Norse culture flourished across Scandinavia during the Viking Age, conventionally dated from 793 CE to 1066
CE. The age is often said to begin with the raid on the monastery of Lindisfarne in 793 CE, when Norse
seafarers from what are now Norway and Denmark attacked the English coast. Over the following centuries Norse
traders, raiders, and settlers ranged from the rivers of the Rus' in the east to the North Atlantic.

The explorer Leif Erikson reached North America around 1000 CE, establishing a short-lived settlement at
Vinland, centuries before Columbus. Norse society was bound together by a rich oral and literary tradition:
the Poetic Edda and the Prose Edda, the latter compiled by the Icelandic scholar Snorri Sturluson around
1220 CE, preserved the mythology of gods such as Odin and Thor. The Icelandic sagas recorded the deeds of
settlers and kings.

The Norse worshipped a pantheon of gods until the gradual Christianization of Scandinavia, which accelerated
after the conversion of kings such as Harald Bluetooth of Denmark in the late tenth century. The Viking Age
is traditionally said to close with the Battle of Stamford Bridge in 1066 CE, where the English king Harold
Godwinson defeated the Norwegian king Harald Hardrada.
