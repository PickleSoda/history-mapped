The Roman Republic was born in 509 BCE, when the patricians of the city of Rome expelled the last king,
Tarquin the Proud, and replaced the monarchy with two annually elected consuls. From its earliest decades the
young Republic was riven by tension between the patricians, who monopolized political and religious office,
and the plebeians, the broad mass of common citizens. This Conflict of the Orders simmered for more than two
centuries, and one of its first great victories came in 451 BCE with the codification of the Twelve Tables,
the foundational legal code inscribed on bronze and displayed in the Forum so that the law could no longer be
hidden from the plebeians. In moments of crisis the Romans also turned to trusted men such as Cincinnatus,
who in 458 BCE was summoned from his plow, made dictator to rescue a beleaguered army, and then famously laid
down his absolute power to return to farming.

As Rome consolidated control over the Italian peninsula, it collided with the great maritime city of Carthage,
and the resulting Punic Wars defined the Republic's rise to Mediterranean dominance. The First Punic War (264
to 241 BCE) wrested Sicily from Carthage, but it was the Second Punic War (218 to 201 BCE) that nearly
destroyed Rome. At the Battle of Cannae in 216 BCE, the Carthaginian general Hannibal annihilated a vast
Roman army in one of the worst defeats the legions ever suffered. Rome recovered, and at the Battle of Zama
in 202 BCE, Scipio Africanus decisively crushed Hannibal in North Africa. The Third Punic War (149 to 146
BCE) ended with the total destruction of Carthage, leaving the Republic the unrivaled power of the western
Mediterranean and flooding Italy with wealth, slaves, and the standardized silver currency of the denarius.

That very expansion strained Roman society, for the small farmers who filled the legions were ruined while
the elite amassed enormous estates. The brothers Tiberius and Gaius Gracchus, known as the Gracchi, attempted
to redistribute public land to the poor. Tiberius was killed by a senatorial mob in 133 BCE and Gaius met a
violent end in 121 BCE, and their deaths opened an era of political bloodshed. The grievances of Rome's
Italian allies, denied full citizenship, erupted into the Social War (91 to 88 BCE), after which citizenship
was at last extended across the peninsula. Out of these convulsions rose the general Sulla, who marched on
Rome and made himself dictator in 82 BCE, proscribing his enemies before stepping down in 79 BCE.

The Republic's final generation belonged to a handful of ambitious commanders. In 60 BCE, Pompey, Crassus,
and Julius Caesar formed the First Triumvirate, an informal diplomatic alliance that allowed them to dominate
the state. Caesar conquered Gaul through the 50s BCE, but after Crassus died and Pompey turned against him,
Caesar crossed the Rubicon with his legions in 49 BCE, igniting civil war. Victorious, he was named dictator
for life, only to be assassinated in the Senate on the Ides of March in 44 BCE by senators fearful that he
would crown himself king.

Caesar's death did not restore the old order. His heir Octavian joined Mark Antony and Lepidus in the Second
Triumvirate, formalized in 43 BCE, which hunted down the assassins at the Battle of Philippi in 42 BCE. The
alliance soon fractured as Antony allied himself with Cleopatra, queen of Egypt. The rivalry was settled at
the naval Battle of Actium in 31 BCE, where Octavian's fleet shattered the forces of Antony and Cleopatra,
who both took their own lives the following year as Egypt was annexed.

With no rival left, Octavian transformed the exhausted Republic into the Roman Empire. In 27 BCE the Senate
granted him the title Augustus, and though he carefully preserved the forms of the old constitution, he ruled
as the first emperor, commanding the loyalty of the legions and reorganizing law, taxation, and provincial
government. When Augustus died in 14 CE, power passed peacefully to his successor Tiberius, confirming that
the Republic had given way for good to a system of imperial rule that would endure for centuries.
