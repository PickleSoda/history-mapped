Epidemic disease has repeatedly reshaped the course of human history. The Plague of Athens struck the city
in 430 BCE during the Peloponnesian War against Sparta, killing perhaps a third of the population and the
statesman Pericles. Centuries later, the Antonine Plague swept the Roman Empire beginning in 165 CE, carried
home by soldiers returning from campaigns in the east.

The Plague of Justinian erupted in 541 CE in the Byzantine Empire under Emperor Justinian I, devastating
Constantinople and the wider Mediterranean world and weakening Justinian's efforts to reconquer the west.
The most catastrophic pandemic was the Black Death, a bubonic plague that reached Europe in 1347 CE aboard
Genoese trading ships docking at the Sicilian port of Messina. By 1351 CE it had killed an estimated third
of Europe's population, shattering the medieval social and economic order.

After 1492 CE the Columbian Exchange carried smallpox and other Old World diseases to the Americas, where
they contributed to the collapse of the Aztec Empire and the Inca Empire. In 1918 CE, near the end of the
First World War, the influenza pandemic known as the Spanish Flu spread across the globe and killed tens of
millions of people.
