The Renaissance was a cultural and intellectual movement that began in Florence in the fourteenth century
CE and spread across Europe, marking a revival of classical Greek and Roman learning. It was sustained by
the patronage of powerful families such as the Medici, who funded artists, architects, and scholars.

Leonardo da Vinci painted the Mona Lisa and the Last Supper, embodying the era's union of art and science.
Michelangelo sculpted the David and painted the ceiling of the Sistine Chapel in Rome between 1508 and 1512
CE. The broader movement of humanism, championed by scholars such as Petrarch and Erasmus of Rotterdam,
emphasized the close study of classical texts and the dignity of human reason.

The invention of the printing press by Johannes Gutenberg around 1440 CE in the city of Mainz dramatically
accelerated the spread of ideas across Europe. In 1543 CE Nicolaus Copernicus published On the Revolutions
of the Heavenly Spheres, proposing a heliocentric model of the cosmos that challenged the medieval worldview
and helped ignite the Scientific Revolution that followed.
