The Silk Road was a network of trade routes connecting China with Central Asia, Persia, the Mediterranean,
and Europe. It took shape during the Han Dynasty of China, after the emperor Han Wudi sent the envoy Zhang
Qian westward around 138 BCE to seek allies against the Xiongnu nomads. His journeys opened regular trade
between China and the kingdoms of Central Asia.

Along these routes flowed silk, spices, paper, and precious metals, but also religions and technologies.
Buddhism spread from India into China along the Silk Road during the first centuries CE. The city of
Samarkand became a great commercial hub, and the oasis towns of the Tarim Basin grew wealthy on the caravan
trade. Under the Tang Dynasty the route reached a peak, linking the Chinese capital of Chang'an with the
markets of Baghdad and Constantinople.

The Mongol Empire of the thirteenth century unified much of Eurasia and secured the routes, a period of
relative safety known as the Pax Mongolica. It was during this time that the Venetian merchant Marco Polo
traveled to the court of Kublai Khan, returning around 1295 CE with accounts that fascinated Europe. The
overland trade declined after the fall of the Mongol Empire and the rise of maritime routes in the fifteenth
century.
