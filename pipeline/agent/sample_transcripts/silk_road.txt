The Silk Road was not a single highway but a vast web of caravan tracks binding China to the Mediterranean, and its story properly begins with a Han Dynasty diplomat. In 138 BCE the emperor Wu dispatched the envoy Zhang Qian westward to seek allies against the nomadic Xiongnu; though captured and held for years, he returned to the Han capital of Chang'an in 126 BCE with knowledge of the kingdoms of Central Asia. His reports prompted the Han opening of the western routes around 114 BCE, and soon Chinese silk, the most coveted of all natural resources, was flowing toward Persia and Rome alongside jade carried east from the oasis quarries of Khotan. By 60 BCE the Han had established the Protectorate of the Western Regions to garrison the desert corridor and guard the merchants who passed through it.

As goods moved, so did beliefs. Buddhism, a religious movement born in India, traveled the caravan roads into China during the first century CE, and tradition holds that the emperor Ming founded the White Horse Temple at Luoyang around 68 CE to house the first Buddhist scriptures. The cave temples of Dunhuang, begun around 366 CE, became a luminous repository of Buddhist art at the desert's edge, while pilgrims such as the monk Xuanzang journeyed west to India between 629 CE and 645 CE to gather sacred texts. Along the way the oasis cities thrived: Samarkand, in the fertile valley of Sogdiana, rose as a glittering hub of commerce, and its Sogdian merchants became the indispensable middlemen of the trade, their language serving as the lingua franca of the road for centuries.

The routes reached their zenith under the Tang Dynasty. After the Tang reunified China in 618 CE, Chang'an swelled into a cosmopolitan capital of perhaps a million people, drawing Persian, Turkic, and Sogdian traders who dealt in spices, horses, and bolts of silk. The Tang clash with the Abbasid Caliphate at the Battle of Talas in 751 CE proved unexpectedly momentous, for Chinese prisoners are said to have carried the technology of paper-making westward into the Islamic world, where a paper mill rose in Baghdad by 794 CE. From there paper spread toward Constantinople and eventually Venice, transforming record-keeping and learning across Eurasia.

Money and machinery followed the same currents. For centuries trade had run on round Chinese coins strung through their square holes, but the demands of long-distance commerce gave rise to the world's first paper money, the jiaozi, issued in Sichuan in the early eleventh century CE. Later inventions journeyed outward too: gunpowder, perfected by Song alchemists, and the magnetic compass, described as a navigational aid in China by the late eleventh century CE, both drifted westward along the trade arteries to reshape warfare and seafaring far beyond their birthplace.

The road's golden afterglow came with the Mongol Empire. After Genghis Khan launched his conquests in 1206 CE, his successors stitched together an empire stretching from Korea to the Danube, and the resulting Pax Mongolica made the routes safer than they had been in generations. It was in this interval that the Venetian merchant Marco Polo set out in 1271 CE, reaching the court of Kublai Khan and serving the Great Khan for years before returning to Venice around 1295 CE to dictate the travels that fired Europe's imagination. The Mongol peace also drew distant cities together, even as Baghdad, sacked by Hulagu Khan in 1258 CE, was eventually folded into a single trading sphere.

Yet the overland network could not last. The breakup of the Mongol realms, the ravages of the Black Death that spread along these very routes around 1347 CE, and the inward turn of Ming China after its founding in 1368 CE all sapped the caravan trade. When Vasco da Gama rounded Africa to reach India by sea in 1498 CE, the cheaper maritime spice routes increasingly eclipsed the dusty land passages, and the long age of the Silk Road, which had carried silk, faith, paper, and money across half the world, slowly gave way to the era of ocean-going commerce.
